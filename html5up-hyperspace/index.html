Recovery Strategies

Currently, NYC is set to receive $7.3 billion in federal aid for school system recovery from COVID-19. NYC plans to spend this aid on the following short-term initiatives:

Operational Support

This is the largest spending category and consists of funding for facilities, charter schools, staff recruitment and retention programs, and school reopenings.

Academic Recovery and Student Supports

This category provides funding for academic assessments, academic interventions such as ELA and math instruction, tutoring, and extended learning time, and the Summer Rising program

Instructional Support

This category provides funding for professional development.

IT Support

This category provides funding to fund IT help desks, enhance school Wi-Fi, and continue wireless broadband service for devices purchased for remote learning.

Write Up

Introduction

The COVID-19 pandemic has had a significant impact on the NYC public education system. In particular, absenteeism (or decreased attendance) has increased in all districts since 2020. In this project, we explore if this issue has impacted different areas and groups in New York, as well as see how absenteeism affects other variables like academic performance. Through interactive data visualizations, we examine heterogeneity in absenteeism across racial and socioeconomic groups and present a convincing (and statistically significant) negative effect of absenteeism on test score performance in NYC public schools.

Related Work

Previous research has established that attendance is critical to strong academic performance. Also, it was also well-documented in 2020 and 2021 that racial minorities and lower-income New Yorkers were hit hardest by the COVID pandemic across a variety of metrics. Our project builds on these findings through a simple but compelling narrative that ties absenteeism to test performance.

Methodology

We use data from the New York City Department of Education, which includes attendance rates, test scores, poverty rates, and racial/ethnic composition for each school district between 2018 and 2022. We use data from 2018 onwards to focus on the window before and after the COVID pandemic, and to only include data that is easily comparable across time (the standardized tests were last redesigned in 2018). To convey the main issue at hand–widespread increasing absenteeism–we created an interactive choropleth map of NYC. This visual shows an across-the-board increase in absenteeism and also hints at unequal effects. To emphasize the importance of increasing absenteeism, we explore the relationship between attendance and test score performance. We run through a simple linear regression analysis to test different components of this relationship, including different effects by demographic group. We are cautious about causal claims, but the strong correlations in this section are clear–absenteeism is associated with lower test scores. By using the same underlying scatterplot in each visual on this page, we hope to reduce the cognitive load needed to remember the data, and save the reader’s mental power for understanding the problem.

Design

Our first visualization is an interactive choropleth map of NYC. Created with bokeh, the visual shows which districts (represented by darker shading) have faced larger increases in absenteeism from 2018 to 2022. Users can hover over the map to see more statistics, like the district’s rate of poverty or ethnic composition. The second visualization page of scatterplots and regressions, created using seaborn, depicts the relationship between test score performance (vertical axis) and attendance rate (horizontal axis). The same scatterplot is shown with different groupings (e.g. color relates to poverty or color relates to ethnic groups) to rigorously test the relationship between scores and attendance. In each subsection, a "Show Regressions" button adds regression lines to the same image and provides more curious readers with additional quantitative context. These visualizations are packaged and displayed in a simple website layout that encourages a sequential view but does not restrict readers that want to jump around. We present visualizations on individual pages to allow readers to digest one arc at a time, within the broader narrative of attendance harming public school students.

Discussion

Our final product demonstrates unequal changes in absenteeism across New York and the relationship between attendance and test scores. Users found the interactive elements engaging and informative, particularly appreciating the ability to manipulate the scatter plots and regression lines. Some users, however, suggested that certain components, like viewing the relationship over time, could be shown more clearly through a chronological animation, which we implemented via a gif that adds regression lines by year for the intertemporal analysis. We also received some feedback that the additional information with the regressions was somewhat overwhelming, so we made that text more concise and put the “Show/Hide Regressions” button in place (as opposed to moving beyond the extra text), so that readers could more easily switch between the two modes.

Future Work

To expand our project, we would explore additional data sources to include factors like school resources, teacher qualifications, and parental involvement. We would also be interested to test our findings and see if they hold up under a microdata-driven analysis, as opposed to merely at the district level. Another area we’d like to explore is the effect of access to public transport on absenteeism and academic achievement.
